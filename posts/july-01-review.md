## Round of 32 Review

## England vs Congo DR

Atlanta opened the knockout stage as first-time participants DR Congo faced off against tournament favorites England. The match set the tone for a day of late, exciting drama. England and Harry Kane scored twice late to rescue a 2-1 win over Les Léopards.

The Leopards got off to a flying start, scoring in the seventh minute and immediately putting England on their heels. I really liked their game plan for the first 15 minutes, where they decided not to sit in a defensive shell and instead played as if they were facing a team of equal stature. They kept the ball, caught England off guard, and stretched the pitch to find an open man in the box who converted the chance. Mexico and future opponents will have noticed this, and it will be interesting to see how they adapt it.

As I mentioned, England can struggle to break down low defensive blocks, and today was no different. Fortunately for them, they have Harry Kane, who is striker "1b" in the tournament next to Erling Haaland, and he showed it today. The first goal was a classy finish, but his second was out of this world. He struck the ball with incredible power while keeping it down just long enough—a display of world-class skill that is extremely difficult to execute.

Unfortunately for Congo, they ran completely out of gas. They subbed off several of their most talented players in search of fresh legs, but the disparity in depth was too large. After going down in the 80th minute, they could not muster much of an attacking threat. Harry Kane is carrying this team through the tournament; his finishing has been superb, just as it has been all year. I would like to see the rest of the team be more creative in providing him with chances moving forward. To be fair, most of the squad hasn't played poorly, but we haven't seen anyone step up to Kane's level. To keep advancing, they will need someone else—whether it is Bellingham, Rice, or one of their many wingers—to step up. They face Mexico next at the Azteca.

Congo should hold their heads high and be incredibly proud. They looked excellent today and throughout the tournament, playing like they belonged on this stage and going toe-to-toe with the best in the world. After Ivory Coast and Morocco, they are the African team that has impressed me the most, and I look forward to seeing them in the future. Good skill, good tactics, and a very proud performance.

## Belgium vs Senegal

If you liked the late drama of the first game, you had to love Belgium and Senegal in Seattle. Senegal took a two-goal lead into the 85th minute, conceded twice in three minutes, and ultimately lost 3-2 in the final minute of extra-time stoppage time by conceding a penalty. This is an all-time collapse and incredibly embarrassing for the reigning African champions.

They looked so good for the first 80 minutes: aggressive, decisive, positive, and creative. They were having fun. Their second goal was beautiful, featuring a long ball followed by a clinical first touch and finish from Ismaïla Sarr that should be shown to anyone learning the game. As a striker, Sarr was excellent and was easily Senegal's best player all tournament.

And then it all faded away. The collapse began almost immediately after the Belgian manager subbed off Kevin De Bruyne and Jérémy Doku—debatably Belgium's two best players, though neither was great today or in the tournament. Surprisingly, the change worked. Belgium played much better and found it easier to progress the ball.

Belgium's two best players of the match—specifically in the final 30 minutes, because it felt like they only showed up for the end—were Youri Tielemans and Romelu Lukaku. Lukaku completely changed the game; his hold-up play was exceptional, and he helped the team exploit the wings. As an American, I ask that Belgium does not start him, but as a neutral fan of the game, he absolutely must start. The team looks far more confident with him on the field. I would be shocked if we do not see lineup changes based on how they looked in the second half and extra time.

For Tielemans, it was a captain's performance, especially after his uncharacteristic spat with teammate Leandro Trossard. His service, positioning, and control of the midfield were stellar. Then, of course, came the two goals. Lukaku handed him the ball for the penalty, and it was arguably the best-taken penalty of the tournament. While it feels terrible to concede a penalty at the very end of extra time, it was absolutely the correct call. It was a clumsy challenge where the defender caught Tielemans on the calf with his full boot.

Senegal played their way out of the tournament, imploding in the final five minutes of normal time and the last 30 seconds of extra time. This does not come down to a single individual or moment; it was a colossal failure of the team as a unit. It was a highly disappointing tournament performance from them overall. They played three European powerhouses and lost to all of them with varying degrees of competitiveness. Many, including myself, expected this team to be a dark horse capable of challenging Norway and France and winning a few knockout games. Instead, they lost to Norway and France relatively convincingly, and then fell to the weakest of the traditional European powers. It felt like they were turning a corner, and they still can, but they will need to make some tough decisions and maintain their focus. This is especially true given that they seem set to lose their CAF court case over the championship.

## United States vs Bosnia-Herzegovina

San Francisco hosted easily the most one-sided game of the day, though it still featured some late drama. Despite going down to 10 men following a clear red card for Folarin Balogun (accidental, but a clear red card—he knows better), the US eased to a 2-0 win. Balogun managed to give the US the lead before his unfortunate dismissal.

Even after the red card, it was hard to tell which team had the man advantage. The US eventually earned a free kick, which Malik Tillman converted, allowing them to comfortably see out the match. To put things in perspective, Bosnia finished with an xG (expected goals) of around 0.14, which is pathetic when playing with a man up. Bosnia was poor today; they looked like a completely different team than the one we had seen leading up to this point. They had no control in possession, never threatened when they had the ball, and were defensively overmatched by the pace and skill on the flanks. It was a disappointing final performance from the Bosnians, and it is likely the last time we will see Edin Džeko on the international stage. The world says goodbye to a legend.

For the US, almost everyone had a good game, save for the goalkeeper who looked a little shaky. I want to highlight both Tyler Adams and Weston McKennie, who served as the engines of the midfield as they have all tournament. They were excellent in possession, pressed effectively, combined well, won the ball back, and worked incredibly hard to track back. It was an excellent performance that makes the entire team look much better.

The US now returns to Seattle—one of the premier soccer cities in the country—to face Belgium, leaving them with a Balogun-sized hole to fill in the lineup on Monday. They have a few options, including subbing in Ricardo Pepi or adjusting the formation, which we can discuss in the upcoming preview.
