## Portugal vs Congo DR

Today marks the final day of the teams' first games. We get to see the final two pre-tournament favorites start today, including one in this game as the Portuguese take on Les Léopards of the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Cristiano Ronaldo is one of a few players making a fifth World Cup appearance. The issue for Portugal is that he might not be one of their best players anymore. He is over 40 years old but isn't really slowing down. He is still dominant in the air—maybe the best at the tournament. His work rate is unmatched as he tracks back frequently, which is an excellent trait for a forward. However, Portugal has so much talent on the team who bring just a bit more to the pitch at this point in their careers—players like Vitinha and Bruno Fernandes. For the DRC, this is a relatively good team that expected to be here when the qualification campaign started. They have a couple of Premier League veterans in Aaron Wan-Bissaka and Yoane Wissa. Expect this team to play similarly to Cabo Verde but with better counter-attacking options. They will challenge Portugal and give them fits. Can they repeat what Cabo Verde did to Spain on Monday? We shall see.

## England vs Croatia

Our last favorite of the tournament, the Three Lions of England, take on everyone's favorite dark horse, the Vatreni of Croatia. This is the final match of the first round of games that pits two of the strongest teams in the tournament against each other. Every year, English fans say they are going to win and that "it's coming home." But spoiler alert: it isn't. Not because they can't, but because they won't. But if they are to win, it will rely on someone other than Harry Kane scoring goals for them. They have plenty of team speed and a balanced midfield, one of the world's best goalkeepers, and they should have strong striking partners for Harry Kane. The defense is fine, but it is certainly not the team's strength. If this roster belonged to any country other than England, I would say they have a chance to win, but it's England. It's never Lupus. As for Croatia, they are transitioning out of their Golden Generation. However, other than the three wise men—Andrej Kramarić, Ivan Perišić, and captain Luka Modrić—the team is actually pretty young. They have a strong footballing culture, and their players are dotted around the top leagues of Europe, mostly in the top-flight leagues of Italy and Germany. This team has a lot of star power outside of Modrić, but if they are to go far, we are going to need to see 2018 Luka Modrić, when he won Player of the Tournament and carried the team to the final.

## Ghana vs Panama

The Black Stars of Ghana take on Los Canaleros of Panama in the penultimate game of the first round of group stage games. Ghana comes in as the lowest-ranked team from Africa to make the tournament (even lower than Cabo Verde, even after their updated ranking). This is a far cry from the 2010 Ghana team, which became the third African side to make the quarterfinals at the World Cup. They do, however, have talent, specifically on the wings. If this team is going to succeed, it will be because they are able to get their best player, Antoine Semenyo, on the ball a lot. Don't be surprised to see him drift into the middle of the pitch to make his presence felt. One special note is that former Arsenal starting midfielder Thomas Partey has been denied entry into Canada ahead of this game due to multiple sexual assault allegations against him in England. As for Panama, this is one of the oldest teams in the tournament. They lack a true star, which is normally quite important for middling-ranked countries to do well. They are going to struggle with the speed and youth of Ghana down the wings. Even though Panama (34) is ranked twice as high as Ghana (73), expect a close match here.

## Uzbekistan vs Colombia

Estadio Azteca hosted the first game of the tournament, and it will also host the final game of round one in the group stage. The White Wolves of Uzbekistan will face off against the "Hippos" of Colombia… OK, they are actually known as Los Cafeteros (the Coffee Growers), but anytime I can remind people that there are wild hippos in Colombia, I do it. If this were chess, the Uzbeks would have a huge advantage, but unfortunately for our lupine friends, we are playing football, where they are significant underdogs. This is their first World Cup appearance, and they will just be happy to be here, looking to emulate the results of both Curaçao (getting a goal) and Cabo Verde (getting a point). This is probably not the best matchup for that, but it wasn't supposed to be good for Curaçao or Cabo Verde either. Most of their players play domestically, but they do have a couple of players in Europe, including one of Lens's regular center-backs. Look for Abdukodir Khusanov to keep them organized in their pack as they defend. This is probably the best position for your best player to be in—being able to keep everyone in line, being strong in the little possession they have, and defending one-on-one when the defense breaks down. They only brought three forwards on the roster, so expect them to sit back and defend. For the Coffee Growers, there is a ton of individual talent on this roster. They have an excellent football culture, and the last time the World Cup was hosted in North America, they were tournament favorites. They are a popular dark horse this year. The team is getting towards the older side but is cycling in some younger talent. They'll be relying on their strong wing play to make some noise at the tournament and will hopefully learn from Spain's mistakes on how to break down strong defensive blocks.
