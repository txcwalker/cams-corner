## Netherlands vs Sweden

Northern European friends meet in the World Cup as Houston hosts the Netherlands and Sweden. This is a very strong and balanced Dutch team taking on a top-heavy Sweden. For the Oranje, I want to see Crysencio Summerville play the entire game. First of all, that's an all-time name. More importantly, he was their best player against Japan regardless of whether he scored or not — confident, playing fast, moving off the ball, and looking like the man most likely to score (and he did). Virgil van Dijk will hold down the back and make it much more difficult for Sweden to score on them than it was against Tunisia.

The biggest point of contention is their manager, who, with 20 minutes left in game one, made a series of substitutions and decisions that can only be described as pathetic at best. When there is that much time left and you are up a goal in the first game of the tournament, you need to be playing to win — not playing to not lose. He is in the hot seat and looks like the biggest hurdle for the Dutch to overcome. For the Swedes, it's tough to evaluate their first game against Tunisia. Of course they looked great, but Tunisia looked awful and their goalkeeper was not enjoying himself, making more than a couple of questionable decisions. Sweden were able to score a lot on the break — not necessarily counter attacking, but taking advantage of mistakes and winning the ball back in midfield in odd-man situations. These opportunities will be practically nonexistent against the Dutch, who have a much stronger midfield than Tunisia. It will be interesting to see if Sweden decides to press, as they are one of the teams most capable of doing it with their forwards and attacking midfield. I assume they will not unless necessary, because the Dutch have some of the calmest defenders and holding midfielders in the tournament — but that is something to look for. I expect a back-and-forth match with the Dutch having a slight edge. That said, if one team wins in a blowout, it'll be the Oranje taking advantage of a lackluster Swedish defense.

## Germany vs Ivory Coast

Game two takes us up to the Great White North in Toronto as the Germans meet the Elephants of Ivory Coast. On paper, this is the game of the day — we get to see a team that cakewalked to a 7–1 win face a much different opponent. Ivory Coast poses some very specific problems that not many teams can match. They are fast and skilled on the edge, physical all over the field, win the ball back really well in their back six, and are happy to rely on individual talent to open up space. Look for Yann Diomandé to continue making waves as he did in game one. They are in a position to make it out of the group after grabbing a crucial three points in their first game against Ecuador. For the Germans, this will be their first real measuring stick of the tournament. They did what they were supposed to against a smaller country and looked great doing it, with the only blemish being a counter-attack goal conceded. I expect they won't struggle with the physicality, but the pace on the outside will be interesting to monitor.

Overall, I think the Germans have too much skill in the middle for the Ivorians to keep up. Look for Musiala and Wirtz to make the difference, being creative in the attacking third. A German victory means they are through to the next round. If Ivory Coast wins, they win the group. I incorrectly asserted yesterday that South Korea had a chance to win the group on goal differential — that is not the case. The first tiebreaker is now head-to-head (as it should be), followed by goal differential. So an Ivorian win locks up the group for them.

## Ecuador vs Curaçao

Game three of the day sees first-time participant Curaçao go up against Ecuador, with both teams in dire need of a win. If there is a winner, the loser is eliminated — and if not, both teams are stuck on one point in an almost impossible position. For Curaçao, this is probably, very marginally, their best chance to get points. That said, this is not a physical Curaçao team going up against one of, if not the most physical teams in the tournament. Curaçao are just happy to be here — they got their first goal and are looking for their first point or possible win. They'll play with nothing to lose, but unfortunately they might be the only team at the tournament who are supremely overmatched in nearly every individual matchup. Even though they are playing with the strength of a whole country (and a lot of Dutch-linked players), that country only has 150,000 people.

For Ecuador, we need to see a convincing win here. In their previous game they were not able to convert the chances they had, and it led to losing at the end on a broken play. While they brought their patented physical style to game one, that was just about all that was working. I want to see them bring the skill they most definitely have. Even though he was okay in game one, I am looking for Moisés Caicedo to lead by example, control the game from the start, and calm the team down — including captain Enner Valencia, who looked a bit shaky. I expect a relatively easy win from Ecuador. There is just too big a talent differential between these two teams.

## Tunisia vs Japan

For the first time in my life, we get to see how a team responds when they fire their coach after the first game of the World Cup, as the Samurai Blue of Japan take on the Eagles of Carthage from Tunisia. Tunisia are looking for everything to be different. They were embarrassed in game one — mistakes all over the park, especially defensively and from the goalkeeper. I wouldn't be shocked to see a change in goal. They also gave the ball up way too easily in midfield, which led to more than one goal and easy counter attacks for Sweden. Hannibal Mejbri — you can't make this stuff up — will need to take charge here, distribute the ball decisively, and put teammates in positions to make a difference.

For Japan, this is a very methodical team — not super fast but extremely technical, with their strength in the middle of the pitch. Daichi Kamada ended up getting the equalizer against the Netherlands (not that he knew much about it), but even before that he was Japan's best player. He does an excellent job distributing and controlling the game for Crystal Palace, and wins the ball in midfield on top of it. He is one of my favorite players to watch and somewhat under the radar. I am expecting the Samurai Blue to prevail relatively easily. There are too many holes to patch on the Tunisian side, and it is anyone's guess what is going on in their camp. The talent disparity is too much — expect a comfortable win for the Japanese.
