## Group Stage Round 2 Review

A full slate of Groups E and F, and we came away with a lot of clarity at the top — and a lot of heartbreak at the bottom. The Dutch announced themselves to the world, Germany found a last-gasp hero, a goalkeeper put on one of the all-time performances, and Japan looked every bit like a serious contender.

## Netherlands vs Sweden

Houston got us started as the Dutch and Swedes squared off in the Space City. Apparently the Swedes liked their game against Tunisia so much they decided to repeat the scoreline — they just found themselves on the wrong end of a 5–1 result. The scoreline does and does not represent the game accurately. Both teams were offensively very strong, as we knew they would be. The difference is the Netherlands were able to get their players into slightly better and slightly easier positions to execute.

In the preview I talked about how the Swedish defense was going to need to step up. Defensively, the reason Sweden struggles is a very poor combination of speed and positioning. It's not that their defenders aren't skilled — they play on some of the top clubs around Europe — they just aren't pure positioners and they are slow. In general, the faster you are, the more you can make up for poor positioning. When you lack in both areas, things become difficult.

That said, there was a tremendous amount of team and individual skill on display from the Dutch. If you want to see an example of a goal that every coach draws up on the whiteboard, go watch the first Dutch goal — or really the first three. Specifically the first one: the ball moves from the goalkeeper to the net in 13 seconds and seven touches, with the keeper taking one and the three other players each having two. That's it. The game looks easy when it flows like this. There are two incredible touches in the move — the first touch by Brobbey, the eventual scorer, is immaculate, laying it off for Reijnders, and the final pass from Gakpo to Brobbey is nanometer-perfect. A simple finish in the end. This goal is educational material at every level and is the goal of the tournament for me so far. I'm going to save a deeper Dutch conversation for later — they have two positive results and looked like they fixed a lot of the issues from game one.

For Sweden, I need to see Anthony Elanga for 90 minutes against Japan. He is absolutely electric. Forget that he scored their only goal today — every time he touched the ball, Sweden got more dangerous. He also had one of the most disgusting pieces of skill that nearly led to a goal. Figure out how to play him with Isak, Ayari, and Gyökeres. Speaking of whom, Yasin Ayari continues to look good. Even though he didn't score, he was one of the better players on the pitch, and it took a couple of big saves to keep him off the scoresheet. Really looking forward to seeing those two play together from the start.

## Germany vs Ivory Coast

Toronto rolled out the red carpet for Ivory Coast and Germany in game two. This was an intense match from the first moment, as Germany had a half-chance inside 15 seconds. They matched the physicality and, to some extent, the pace of the Ivorians. Ivory Coast scored first thanks to a great move by Yann Diomandé down the flank finding an open teammate, and a comedy of errors from both teams allowed captain Franck Kessié to finish for the Elephants. Both teams had plenty of chances to add to the scoreboard but couldn't put it together until German sub Denis Undav — calling him the U-boat from now on — finished off a move late in the second half to level it.

The game remained tied with both teams having chances until the very end. Ivory Coast had a sparkling opportunity to seal it in stoppage time, but Adingra couldn't get his feet right in the box and was unable to get a shot away — he must be shooting first time there. A moment later, an Nmecha-to-Undav connection delivered the winner for Germany. Whenever the finish is the easiest touch of the entire move, it's a great sign, and the pass from Nmecha and the first touch from Undav were perfect — a needle of a pass into a delicate first touch and a simple finish. It seems impossible to leave this guy out of the starting lineup going forward.

Both goalkeepers had good days, specifically Fofana in the Ivorian goal. Yes, Germany had a few goals called back, but he made some key saves. Germany have already qualified for the next round and will win the group, so don't put any stock into the lineup in their final game against Ecuador. For Ivory Coast, they cannot relax, but they face Curaçao next — a team Germany beat 7–1 — and only need a draw to advance. It should be a comfortable win in the end.

## Ecuador vs Curaçao

Two teams who lost their first games took to the field in Kansas City with dreams of three points as Ecuador took on Curaçao. This was a pretty entertaining 0–0 draw, and if you like insane goalkeeping sequences and top-notch work from the gloves, this was the game for you. Curaçao keeper Eloy Room had 15 saves, the most in a World Cup game since FIFA started keeping that stat in 1996. Incredible performance from him. Some were routine, but many were not. Not to be outdone, Ecuadorian goalkeeper Hernán Galíndez had a couple of massive moments where less alert goalkeeping would have meant a loss and elimination for Ecuador.

Ecuador have now passed Türkiye for the most expected goals without actually scoring a goal — an xG of just over three today and obviously fell a bit short of that mark. Not a place you want to be. For the second game in a row, Moisés Caicedo was a menace on the field, but he cannot do it all. He needs help, specifically from the attack. For Curaçao, they earned their first point ever at their first World Cup. I can't say they deserved it because they were thoroughly outplayed, but they absolutely earned it on Room's performance alone. They are playing with house money — first goal and first point in their first World Cup. Can they make it out of the group? They would need a win against a very strong Ivory Coast side. Seems hard. Ecuador probably has a better chance against Germany. But look — they have done wonders for soccer culture on the island. This team will be remembered forever.

## Japan vs Tunisia

Monterrey hosted its second game of the World Cup, and for the second time they got to see Tunisia get absolutely obliterated — 5–1 to Sweden in game one, 4–0 against Japan here. Shockingly, firing your manager in the middle of the World Cup probably wasn't the best decision. Maybe, just maybe, it's the players and not the manager. I'm not going to say too much about Tunisia. Former Premier League midfielder Mikel John Obi said it best post-broadcast: they simply are not good enough — as a team and as individuals. Lazy, predictable, and uninspiring. They at least get out of Monterrey for their final game, but they will face the Netherlands, which is a case of a moveable object meeting an unstoppable force.

For Japan, they looked good against the Netherlands and great here. We'll temper expectations a bit because they smoked the worst team in the tournament, but you can only play who is in front of you. This game had a bit of everything: possession, aggression, individual skill, chemistry, and team skill. They showed they have a lot in the bag. The one thing I want to highlight is their team chemistry. It seemed like every player knew where all ten teammates were at all times — whether they're talking, constantly scanning, or just have that feel for each other, I'm not sure, but it's a great sign. They moved with anticipation, had some cheeky blind passes, and put the ball exactly where their teammate wanted it. They are not mathematically guaranteed to advance yet, but with a +4 differential and four points, I am going ahead and saying they are through. Japan have never won a knockout stage game and have an excellent opportunity here. Tunisia have been eliminated from the World Cup, though they do still have to play that last game against the Dutch.
