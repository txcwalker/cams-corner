## Argentina vs Austria

In the first game of the day, two countries whose names both start and end with "A" face off as Austria takes on Argentina in Dallas. If you want a fun geography challenge, try to name a country that starts with "A" but doesn't end in "A" — hint: the other "A" countries in this tournament also end with "A," namely Australia and Algeria.

Argentina had an excellent start to the tournament as Messi dodged a certain red card and completed his hat trick in a 3–0 win. I know it looked like he was the only player on the field, but this Argentina team is like a more refined version of Sweden: incredible offensive options, suspect defense. Luckily, this didn't matter too much in game one, and it shouldn't really matter in this group at all — they probably have the weakest group of any top contender.

Speaking of which, their opponent Austria beat Jordan 3–1 in their first game. If it is possible to look worse and still win 3–1, that is what Austria did. Jordan moved the ball around with ease, and Austria didn't create nearly enough chances for a team that allegedly has more talent. Their first game was a perfect depiction of what possession without intent looks like. So how does Austria knock off the world champs? Well, they don't. But if they do, I'm looking at their stars. David Alaba will need to keep the backline organized and disciplined — no chasing ghosts, no reckless tackles, no biting on pump fakes. Marcel Sabitzer will need to control the game in possession, drop a little deeper, give the team a breather, and get the pace right. They should press when there are numerical advantages, but slow the game down to make Argentina chase, allowing the defenders to recover. I don't expect this to be the case, and I see Argentina cruising to another easy 3–0.

## France vs Iraq

In theory this should be an absolute whooping as France and Iraq travel to Philly. France looked completely anemic and uninspired in the first half of game one, then flipped a switch and looked like potential world champs in the second half of a 3–1 win against Senegal. Even when their offense looks stale, they have two of the best holding midfielders and centerbacks in the world and win the ball back often and convincingly. I expect a more one-sided game than Iraq–Norway — partially because that was competitive in the first half, and partially because France, for me, is the best team in the world. They only used two subs against Senegal; I expect to see all five here, and don't be shocked to see a few lineup changes to get more players up to game speed. The one player who did not look good in game one was goalkeeper Mike Maignan — uncharacteristic for him, and I expect a bounce back, but a situation to monitor.

For Iraq, how do you win this? Realistically, you don't. This would be a bigger upset than Cabo Verde drawing Spain. What you need to do is limit the damage and look to upset Senegal in your final game. Don't go in thinking you're already down a couple — go in knowing a couple of goals is possible and that it's okay. Execute your game plan, scratch and claw, and convert on the few chances you get. This World Cup is a learning experience for the players, coaches, and country — valuable reps against the world's best to continue building the foundation and domestic soccer culture. If Iraq has any outside chance of winning, they need an early goal off a mistake, corner, or pinball in the box, then put all 11 on the goal line for 90 minutes and hope France can't score. I think this will be the biggest blowout of the day. 5–0 France.

## Norway vs Senegal

The game of the day takes place in New York as the battle for second place in Group I will likely be determined here. This sets up as one of the matchups of the tournament, and you can bet tensions will be high as both teams understand the implications. For Norway, they won their first game against Iraq relatively easily — though the 4–1 scoreline was misleading for how competitive the game actually was. Senegal comes in experiencing the exact opposite emotions, unable to capitalize on French mistakes in the first half and ending up with a 3–1 loss. Senegal needs at least a point to avoid being all but eliminated, while Norway can afford a loss but would then need a draw against France to stay in the tournament.

Let's break down a couple of the key matchups. First, I'll be looking at the Norwegian attack — specifically Haaland and Sørloth — and how they impose their will on the Senegalese centerbacks. Sørloth is more of a classic hold-up striker, but Haaland poses problems for every team with his combination of power and speed. The midfield battle will also be fascinating. The Norwegian midfield is a methodical build-up unit that wants to play half-court. I wouldn't say any team likes to defend in transition, but Norway has good players for it — Sander Berge is one of the better holding midfielders in the Premier League, and most of their back seven can legally stop counters and start the other way.

For Senegal, their style is very different. They want to play fast, with plenty of pace and creativity in midfield, looking to get out in transition and use their skill and speed. Even though their starting midfielders play mostly defensive positions at club level, they have high work rates and get forward. They will need to balance their forward rotations so they don't get caught on the counter against Haaland. One last point: Martin Ødegaard can play very similarly to Michael Olise, who opened the game up in the second half for France. We haven't really seen it yet from him, but Ødegaard has a long track record as a creative force for Arsenal. If he is able to pass players open, it is going to be a long day for Senegal. This Senegal team is no joke and I expect them to compete like their tournament lives depend on it — because they do. That said, I think the most likely result is a draw, as both teams understand the stakes. 2–2.

## Algeria vs Jordan

Our final game of the day takes us to Santa Clara as Jordan battles Algeria to round out Round 2 action in Group J. I was really impressed by Jordan's effort against Austria in their first-ever World Cup game, and it seems desperately unfair that they were unable to get points in that matchup. They have an approximately similar skill matchup against the Fennecs, and I expect a hyper-competitive battle. I can't say I know a lot about this Jordanian team other than from watching game one. To me, it looked like they got a little disorganized at the back under pressure from time to time, especially on set pieces — not constant, but frequent enough to notice. They did, however, move forward really well, and I was impressed with their ability to break the press and get the ball to their attack-minded players. They will need to be more clinical in the final third to get points here, but based on what I saw, it is well within the realm of possibility.

For Algeria, they were a bit unlucky to face a well-rested Messi in game one. They actually played okay, but just let Messi run wild a bit too frequently — slipping his mark more than once and scoring three times. I don't think they played terribly, but mentally, dropping Messi in coverage and then not having a teammate pick him up is a compounding of mental mistakes that should never happen. They need to clean this up if they want to have a chance at advancing. The good news is they still have two non-Argentina matchups left and are more than capable of advancing from the group. They have strong attacking options, the whole team is relatively young, and they have plenty of players at big clubs in Europe — the talent is there. This has the potential to be a sneaky fun, back-and-forth game — or a spot where Algeria, out of the shadow of Messi, really lets loose. I am predicting the former and maybe a baby upset/surprise for Jordan. 2–1 Jordan.
