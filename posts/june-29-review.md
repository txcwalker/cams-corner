## Round of 32 Review

## Brazil vs Japan

Game 1 of the first full day of the knockouts began in Houston with Brazil taking on Japan. Despite making every World Cup since 1998, Japan had yet to win a knockout game, and that did not change here. Brazil scored at the death of regular time to eke out a 2–1 win.

Japan surprised everyone by scoring first on an excellent finish from outside the box. Something worth keeping an eye on is goals scored in transitions off turnovers — each team had one, and that was what led to Japan's opener off a careless Brazilian giveaway in the middle. Brazil equalized from a nice cross into the box in the second half. This game could have had the goal of the tournament from Vinícius Jr. if it weren't for what is now a serious contender for save of the tournament from Zion Suzuki. The game-winner from Martinelli also came off a turnover in Japan's final third, with a pass slipped through on the edge of the offside line — excellent first touch, and a second touch good enough to place it past the goalkeeper, who got a hand to it.

This might have been Brazil's best chance to lose, and they are continuing to grow in confidence. The midfield was excellent again, especially Casemiro and Guimarães. Each win fuels them further as they build momentum. On Japan's side, they stayed organized and disciplined, limiting their mistakes, but Brazil was able to punish them right at the end. Their goalkeeper was outstanding, keeping them in it the entire way.

On Japan's broader outlook — they have made every World Cup since '98 and haven't won a single knockout round. There feels like a pretty significant mental block. This was the first time they had made back-to-back knockouts, and they should be mainstays in the knockout stages given their weak qualifying region. They started the J.League in 1993 as part of a 100-year plan to be dominant in international football. It started strong, but it feels like it has stagnated. They have players across the top clubs and leagues now, but it has not translated to the international stage. They were unlucky to draw Brazil here, but a team of their quality should be getting to the last eight more frequently.

## Germany vs Paraguay

In what was supposed to be the most one-sided game of the day, Germany and Paraguay met in Boston. But after extra time, Paraguay is paragliding into the Round of 16. Classic possession without intent.

Paraguay is a very stingy team defensively — atrocious to watch, but effective. They only gave up 10 goals in 18 qualifying games, tied for second-least in CONMEBOL with Argentina (Ecuador only let in 5). They also only scored 14, tied with Ecuador for the fewest among CONMEBOL teams that qualified. The Paraguay that showed up was ready to defend — they always are — and they did it very successfully. The key was preventing an early goal and getting comfortable in the game, and not only did they do that, they scored first.

Germany equalized through Havertz just after the break. What was most frustrating from a neutral perspective is that Germany looked completely out of ideas. For a team full of top talent across every major league, there was a severe lack of creativity. Even the goal was relatively tame — great technique, but standard. The game lacked a true moment of quality from Germany: a key pass, a 1v1 won, a shot from distance.

Two controversial items to address. First, the goal called back in extra time for goalkeeper interference. Extremely controversial. When Peter Schmeichel — who won Euro 1992, was Manchester United's goalkeeper for the whole of the '90s, and is on the Goalkeepers' Union — says it's not a foul, that carries some weight. For me, the Paraguayan keeper goes down far too easily, and the contact does not impact his ability to play the ball (he was never getting there anyway). However, not so fast. All players were told before the tournament this would be called a foul. Anton has his hands on the keeper — it's a foul, no questions asked, regardless of intent. I hate the call, but they are trying to remove goalkeeper crowding from the game and this is how they are enforcing it.

That said, the referee had a rough day regardless of that decision. This was the most physical game we have seen yet; Musiala probably should have had a red card late. The ref lost control early and never regained it. But the game still went to penalties, and Germany lost fair and square. It is a skill, and players you expect to convert missed. Germany only has themselves to blame. Too much talent to lose to this quality of team. No creativity, no urgency, no magic. In hindsight you could see it coming — great against Curaçao, barely beat Ivory Coast, then lost to Ecuador. When challenged to break down defensive blocks, they couldn't do it.

Paraguay advances, and their reward is playing the winner of France vs. Sweden. They play an incredibly boring brand of football. Do they deserve to advance? Yes. Is it good for the World Cup? Not really. Just another team to be steamrolled on the road to the final.

## Morocco vs Netherlands

We said goodbye to both Monterrey as a host city and the Netherlands, who lost to Morocco on penalties after a 1–1 draw. Both teams had genuine expectations of winning this tournament coming in.

Before the negatives, the Dutch keeper Bart Verbruggen had an excellent night — we have a legitimate contender for save of the tournament. But the Moroccans were the better team on the night. The Dutch held the lead going into stoppage time, and an excellent ball in from a substitute who had just come on led to the equalizer, and then ultimately the win in penalties.

The Dutch tactics need to be discussed. The formation choice was strange — a back five with four centerbacks, only two midfielders, and real lack of support for Summerville and Gakpo out wide. They had very few chances and capitalized on one, scored by Gakpo and assisted by Summerville. They lost this game playing a completely different brand of football than they had all tournament and throughout qualifying. I understand Morocco is a great team — 7th vs. 8th in FIFA rankings at kickoff — but you cannot go into a defensive shell, take away your best players' strengths, and just hope Morocco doesn't score. This was most apparent in extra time, when Morocco must have had 90% possession as the Dutch sat back and didn't press the issue.

That said, the Atlas Lions didn't really press the issue in extra time either. Neither team was creative or applying real pressure — both waiting for the other to make multiple mistakes. The Dutch manager overcooked this one badly, giving his chefs bad ingredients. Expect him to be fired shortly.

For Morocco, they get a much easier game against Canada. It was an exciting game where either team could have won — but disappointing from both sides in terms of attacking pressure, and especially disappointing from the Dutch for abandoning their identity entirely.
